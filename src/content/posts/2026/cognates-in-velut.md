# Cognates in velut

My Latin dictionary, [velut](https://www.velut.co.uk), lists words that have the same etymological root as the word you’ve looked up (cognates).
I’m improving this feature by allowing words to have more than one root.
A list of cognates is then generated for each root of any word you look up.

Most of the roots are given as simple Latin words, which are rendered as links so you can look up that word in the dictionary.

## Lexicography

What roots am I including?
I ignore some elements that are Latin, and I include some roots from languages other than Latin (such as Ancient Greek).

### Prefixes and suffixes

I generally do not include prefixes or suffixes as roots.
There are so many verbs prefixed with <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/ad">ad</a></q>, for example, that including them all as cognates of each other would be unwieldy.
The cognates I do show for <q lang="la">ad</q> are the adverbs and conjunctions where the <q lang="la">ad</q> element doesn’t seem to be a prefix in the same way.

This does have the result that <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/adeo-">adeō</a></q> as an adverb counts as a cognate of <q lang="la">ad</q>, but <q lang="la">adeō</q> as a verb does not (because it’s the verb <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/eo-">eō</a></q> with a prefix).
The noun <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/adverbium">adverbium</a></q> (“adverb”) also doesn’t count as a cognate of <q lang="la">ad</q>.

Numeric prefixes (ūni-, bi-, tri-, etc) do count towards the roots.
They are related to the numerals <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/u-nus">ūnus</a></q>, <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/duo">duo</a></q>, <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/tre-s">trēs</a></q>, etc.
Less obviously, the prefix sēmi- (“half”) and its Greek equivalent hēmi- are also related to <q lang="la">ūnus</q>.

Fun fact: <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/vi-ginti-">vīgintī</a></q> (“twenty”) comes from words for “two” and “ten”, so it’s cognate to both <q lang="la">duo</q> and <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/decem">decem</a></q>.
I only realised this when going through the roots for velut.
The word for twelve <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/duodecim">duodecim</a></q> comes from the same roots, but was apparently created later than <q lang="la">vīgintī</q>, so doesn’t have the morphological changes in Proto-Indo-European that make <q lang="la">vīgintī</q> look different to <q lang="la">duo decem</q>.
This also seems to be true for the other multiples of ten (30–90) and the teen numbers 13–17.

I also count some suffixes as roots where there’s an obvious lemma to put for the root.
For example, the adjective suffixes -fer and -ger are related to the verbs <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/fero-">ferō</a></q> and <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/gero-">gerō</a></q> respectively.
So <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/bicorniger">bicorniger</a></q> (“bearing two horns”) has three sets of cognates.

English-speakers sometimes imagine that adjectives such as “variable” and “detestable” come from the word “able”; or that the Latin sources <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/varia-bilis">variābilis</a></q>, <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/de-testa-bilis">dētestābilis</a></q>, etc come from the Latin <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/habilis">habilis</a></q>.
This is not so.
The suffix is just a suffix.
Latin <q lang="la">habilis</q> comes from the verb <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/habeo-">habeō</a></q> and a suffix (either -bilis or -ilis).
Other adjectives like <q lang="la">variābilis</q> and <q lang="la">dētestābilis</q> come from verbs and the suffix -bilis, but they do not come from <q lang="la">habilis</q> and I therefore do not list <q lang="la">habeō</q> as a root for them.

### Greek roots

If part of a compound word derives from Ancient Greek, I try to find a simple (ie, not compound) Latin word that is related.
If there isn’t one, I find an Ancient Greek word to use for the root.

For instance, ‘<a lang="la" href="https://www.velut.co.uk/rhi-nocero-s">rhīnocerōs</a>’ (“rhinoceros”) has parts that are derived from Greek <q lang="grc">ῥίς</q> (“nose”) and <q lang="grc">κέρας</q> (“horn”).
The second part is related to the Latin <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/cornu-">cornū</a></q>, but the first part does not exist in Latin.
So on velut I display a list of cognates for <q lang="grc">ῥίς</q> and a list of cognates for <q lang="la">cornū</q>.

### Roots from other languages

Some of my lemmata are not related to either Latin or Greek roots.
<q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/I-slandia">Īslandia</a></q> (“Iceland”) has parts that are related to the English “ice” and “land”, but they do not have cognates in Latin.
Likewise, several names derive from Semitic languages (such as Hebrew or Akkadian), which makes their parts especially unlikely to have Latin cognates.
I haven’t totally decided how to deal with this, but I’m investigating.

The best idea I’ve had would be to store each root as a word with a language-code (eg `"EN ice"` or `"EN land"`).
On the website, this could be rendered to HTML as a word with a language attribute — such as `<span lang="EN">ice</span>`.
I might even display a little flag next to the word, like I already do when a lemma has transliterations in <span><img class="inline-flag" alt="Flag of Greece" src="https://www.velut.co.uk/images/greece.png" /> Ancient Greek</span> or <span><img class="inline-flag" alt="Flag of Israel" src="https://www.velut.co.uk/images/israel.png" /> Hebrew</span>.

(Don’t ask me what flag I’d use for Akkadian.
Maybe the flag of modern Iraq?
I’m already using the flags of modern Greece and Israel for Ancient Greek and Hebrew, so I’m not always averse to anachronism!)

## Software changes

But what changes have I made to my code to get roots (plural) appearing in velut?

velut used to be an Excel file, with a `Root` column on the `lemmata` table (the table of dictionary headwords).
To construct a list of cognates for a given lemma, it was easy to look up all the lemmata that had the same `Root` value, and I had formulae for this.
For this to work, each lemma’s root would be either the lemma itself or another lemma, but if a lemma was the root of another lemma, it must be its own root.
(Essentially, if the root of <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/verba-tim">verbātim</a></q> is <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/verbum">verbum</a></q>, the root of <q lang="la">verbum</q> must also be <q lang="la">verbum</q>.
Then any entry whose root is <q lang="la">verbum</q> is a cognate.)

So when I created the velut website, reading the data in Json format, each Json object representing a lemma had a `Root` field whose value was a simple string referring to a lemma.
(Or the field was null.
I didn’t always have a root specified for every lemma.
Nowadays all my lemmata have at least one root given.)

If the field wasn’t null, the website used this `Root` field to construct its lists of cognates.

When I [migrated to Json and JavaScript](./deexcellation-of-velut) and stopped using Excel for my Latin dictionary, I kept the logic for `Root` the same.
There was so much going on with the transition away from Excel that I didn’t want to fiddle with the cognate-finding at the same time.

But of course, I knew that there were many lemmata that are compounds, made of parts deriving from different roots.
For example, <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/ve-riverbium">vēriverbium</a></q> has elements coming from <q lang="la"><a href="https://www.velut.co.uk/ve-rus">vērus</a></q> and <q lang="la">verbum</q>.
Since I only had one root in the data, the <q lang="la">vērus</q> was getting used and the <q lang="la">verbum</q> wasn’t accounted for.

Therefore, I started adding a `Roots` field (plural) to some lemmata, and made sure that the new field was getting included in the MongoDB database that the website reads from.
I didn’t want to break anything before the replacement was in place.

Next, I adapted the website to look for a `Roots` field.
If there was no `Roots`, it used the `Root` field.
For every root that was provided, it generated a list of cognates (lemmata with the same root among its `Roots`/`Root` properties).

I ensured that the front-end looked good for lemmata with no specified roots, one root, and more than one root.
If the root is Latin, it renders as a link; if the root is [Greek](#greek-roots), it’s not a link.

<time datetime="2026-03-02">Then</time> I gave a `Roots` field and an extra field to all lemmata that didn’t yet have `Roots` (13,804 lemmata).
The new `Roots` field was simply derived from the `Root` field in square brackets (to make it a Json array).
The extra field was to say that the `Roots` property would need to be checked.

<!--(The Git commit for that has 27,608 lines added and 14 lines deleted, since 13,804 lemmata were getting two new fields each.)-->

I refreshed the database with the new fields, so I was able to simplify the website logic so it only looks for `Roots` (not `Root`).
Having done this and tested it (manually), I deleted the old `Root` properties.

### Automated tests

In the Excel file, I had formulae checking that the `Root` value (if specified) of every lemma had itself for its root.
This was very useful, and I replicated it in my JavaScript when I was doing that migration.
I have a script that loops over lemmata to construct the inflected forms, and as it did so, it would also check that the `Root` value was itself a lemma and that this lemma had itself for its root.

Now that I have a `Roots` property that is an array, the script checks that every value in `Roots` is either itself a lemma that has itself as a root, or is a string of Greek characters.
It logs to the console whenever a lemma doesn’t have a valid `Roots` field, so I see the mistake easily.

## Adding more roots

At time of writing, all my lemmata have a `Roots` property that the website uses to construct lists of cognates.
Additionally, most of my lemmata also have a property telling me to check the `Roots` property!

So I am going through my lemmata to review their roots.
I’ve started with the longest words — my reasoning is that the longer a word is, the likelier it is to be a compound and thus be missing roots.
I’ve already checked all the lemmata longer than eleven letters.
(However, I will need to go back to some of the words that derive from [languages other than Latin and Greek](#roots-from-other-languages).)

This is a bit like when I went through all my lemmata to check [what inflected forms were being generated](./deexcellation-of-velut#checking-the-inflectors-output-part-1) for them.
That took me twenty-five months.
Reviewing the roots will also take months, but it won’t be as many months!
I’m already down to 10,722 lemmata to check.
I should be done by the end of the year 2026.

Then you’ll be able to look up any word in velut and not be missing out on any cognates (that I know about)!
