# “Electricity” in Latin

My Latin dictionary, [velut](https://www.velut.co.uk), is ten years old!

It’s a project which I had originally started because I was trying to translate pop songs into Latin and I wanted help with making the words rhyme.
And indeed velut has proven to be very useful for listing rhymes.
However, I found my work on the dictionary to be so much fun and so educational — both for learning Latin vocabulary and for practising software development — that the song translations stopped being a priority for me.
I made a Latin version of a Taylor Swift song in September 2017 (“Look What You Made Me Do” or <q lang="la">Fēcistī ut facerem</q>), but didn’t do any more since.

So, to celebrate the start of velut’s second decade, I have used it to translate another song into Latin.

The song I picked is “Electricity” from the musical “Billy Elliot”, lyricized by Lee Hall and composed by Elton John.
It contains several similes, and my translation contains various ways of rendering them in Latin, such as the word <q lang="la">velut</q> itself!

My translation fits the tune of the original, with a stress-based metre as is normal in modern English.
I am not employing elision.
Long vowels are marked in the text, but I’ve used them more for deciding what words will rhyme than for trying to match a length-based metre.
This lets me strike a good balance between the three things I want my translation to be faithful to: the rhyme-scheme, the metre, and the meaning.

Below, you can read my [Latin translation](#my-latin-version), the translation of my Latin text [back into English](#back-translation), and the [original lyrics](#original-text) by Lee Hall.
I also wrote some [footnotes](#footnotes) on specific phrases.

## Back-translation

<blockquote>

_I just ask, William, what do you feel when you dance?_

I can’t explain, <br/>since I lack the words.
I’m besieged by a wild feeling.
As I suppose, I’m forgetting <br/>who I am, not mindful,
but I don’t know how <br/>at the same time I’m being made whole.

As if some sort of music is <br/>very near to me,
and I’m attentive, and I’m attentive — <br/>and then I’m somewhere else.

And the feeling is new, <br/>like it pierces deep inside.
Something bursts me completely, <br/>and very clearly,
suddenly on wings elevates <br/>me like a bird,
like electricity, electricity,
giving fires to me, <br/>and freeing me.

It’s somewhat close to anger, <br/>or similar to fear,
confused and fully mixed, <br/>turbulent.
As if I’ve cried profusely, <br/>I’m empty and full,
but I don’t know what it is; <br/>it’s uncertain.

As if some sort of music is <br/>very near to me —
but I’m attentive to a music silent <br/>and inaudible!

But this feeling moves me, <br/>like it burns me deep inside.
Something bursts me completely, <br/>and very clearly,
suddenly on wings elevates <br/>me like a bird,
like electricity, electricity,
giving fires to me, <br/>and freeing me.

Freeing.

</blockquote>

## Original text

<blockquote>

_Can I just ask you, Billy, what does it feel like when you’re dancing?_

I can’t really explain it: <br/>I haven’t got the words.
It’s a feeling that you can’t control.
I suppose it’s like forgetting, <br/>losing who you are,
but at the same time <br/>something makes you whole.

It’s like that there’s a music, <br/>playing in your ear,
and I’m listening, and I’m listening — <br/>and then I disappear.

And then I feel a change, <br/>like a fire deep inside,
something bursting me wide open, <br/>impossible to hide,
and suddenly I’m flying, <br/>flying like a bird,
like electricity, electricity
sparks inside of me, <br/>and I’m free, I’m free.

It’s a bit like being angry; <br/>it’s a bit like being scared,
confused and all mixed up and <br/>mad as hell.
It’s like when you’ve been crying <br/>and you’re empty and you’re full,
but I don’t know what it is; <br/>it’s hard to tell.

It’s like that there’s a music, <br/>playing in your ear —
but the music is impossible, <br/>impossible to hear!

But then I feel it move me, <br/>like a burning deep inside,
something bursting me wide open, <br/>impossible to hide,
and suddenly I’m flying, <br/>flying like a bird,
like electricity, electricity
sparks inside of me, <br/>and I’m free, I’m free.

Free, I’m free.

</blockquote>

## Footnotes

<ol>
<li id="fn1"><p>
I quickly settled on a translation for the first line, but I had several options for the second.
For a while I was quite fond of <q lang="la">Rātiōne nōn contineor</q> (“I am not constrained by reason”).
Then reason got the better of me and I changed the line to something less psychopathic.
</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn1">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn2"><p>
Lee Hall’s lyrics make a lot of use of the generic second person — eg, “it’s a feeling that you can’t control” meaning that people in general can’t control the feeling.
But Billy is narrating his own experience, so he could have said “it’s a feeling that <em>I</em> can’t control”.
My translation leans more in that direction — there are no second-person verbs in my Latin here!
The second person can be used generically in Latin (especially with subjunctive verbs), but it’s not as common as in colloquial English.
I think my Latin translations sound cleaner when there isn’t the inconsistency of some verbs being first-person and some verbs being second-person when they could be first-person.
</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn2">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn3"><p>
I was hopeful that the verb <q lang="la">attendō</q> could translate as both “I’m attentive” and “I’m in attendance”</id>, for a nice pun before <q lang="la">sum tunc alibī</q>, but the latter meaning isn’t quite there in the Latin.
There’s no pun in the English, of course, so my <q lang="la">attendō et attendō</q> is still an adequate translation of “I’m listening and I’m listening”, even if my attempt at wordplay was more anticlimactic than antanaclasic.
</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn3">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn4"><p>
There are two similes here back-to-back, but one is describing Billy (“like a bird”) and the other is describing whatever’s making him fly (“like electricity”).
Latin’s grammatical cases make the distinction clearer: “me like a bird” is the object of the sentence (accusative) and “something… like electricity” is the subject (nominative).
</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn4">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn5">
<p>
<q lang="la">ēlectricitās</q> is not classical Latin, but it is the usual word for electricity in modern Latin.
Besides, <q lang="la">ut ēlectricitās, ēlectricitās</q> fits the metre here beautifully: both in terms of stress and in terms of syllable length (short long long short short long, long long short short long)!
</p>
<p>
Perhaps the rhyme suffers a little from <q lang="la">mihi dāns</q> having a nasal “ā” vowel that is not nasal in <q lang="la">ēlectricitās</q> (in IPA: <code>[ˈmɪ.ɦɪ dãːs]</code> vs <code>[eː.ɫɛkˈtrɪ.kɪ.taːs]</code>), and then <q lang="la">līberāns</q> has different vowels again.
I could have mitigated that by making <q lang="la">ēlectricitās</q> into a vocative, rhyming with <q lang="la">mihi dās</q> and <q lang="la">līberās</q> — as in, “O electricity, you give me fires and you free me!”
But talking explicitly to the electricity would have been a bit silly and I wanted to keep <q lang="la">ēlectricitās</q> nominative (for the <a href="#fn4">simile</a>).
</p>
<p>(My transcriptions in the International Phonetic Alphabet are derived from Wiktionary and might not be fully accurate, and indeed are based on classical Latin when I’ve already said <q lang="la">ēlectricitās</q> is not classical Latin.
Nonetheless, they should give a sense of how good the rhyme is.)</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn5">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn6">
<p>
Now, what tense should <q lang="la">lacrimārim</q> be?
If Billy is imagining that he’s still crying when he’s feeling empty and full, then it would be present tense, eg <q lang="la">lacrimō</q>/<q lang="la">lacrimem</q>. But otherwise, the perfect tense of <q lang="la">lacrimāvī</q>/<q lang="la">lacrimārim</q> is a better translation (“I’ve been crying and have now stopped but there might be some resultant effect on my current state”).
The imperfect <q lang="la">lacrimābam</q>/<q lang="la">lacrimārem</q> is also possible, but doesn’t have the emphasis on the resultant state (it’s a plain “I was crying” rather than “I have been crying [and it makes me feel simultaneously empty and full]”).
</p>
<p>As for the mood (indicative/subjunctive), I prefer the subjunctive here, which has more of a vibe of something hypothetical or abstract than the indicative mood would.
In English, the generic “you” gives some of that abstract quality, but I’m not using the generic second person in my Latin, so I chose to change the mood to subjunctive.</p>
<p>If I had gone with a trisyllabic verb (<q lang="la">lacrimō</q>/<q lang="la">lacrimem</q>) instead of a tetrasyllabic <q lang="la">lacrimārim</q>, I would have needed a longer adverb than <q lang="la">largē</q> to pad out the line, eg <q lang="la">Ceu lacrimem longinquō</q> (“As if I’ve been crying for a long time”).</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn6">Back to text</a></p>
</li>

<li id="fn7"><p>
A pentasyllabic rhyme <q lang="la">ūrit intimē / lūcidissimē</q>? Yes please!
</p>
<p class="back-to-text"><a href="#link-fn7">Back to text</a></p>
</li>
</ol>
